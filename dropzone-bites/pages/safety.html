Skydiving Safety Guidelines

Welcome to the safety hub of DropZone Bites! Whether you're a first-time jumper or an experienced skydiver, following the correct procedures ensures your safety and that of others. Below is your essential guide.

1. Arrival at the Dropzone

  • Arrive early to attend the morning briefing.
  • Check in at manifest and confirm your jump ticket.
  • Review weather updates and safety notices.

2. Equipment Check

  • Conduct a full gear inspection before each jump:
    • Inspect main and reserve containers.
    • Verify AAD (Automatic Activation Device) is armed.
    • Check leg straps, chest strap, 3-ring system.
  • Perform a buddy check with another jumper.

3. Aircraft Boarding

  • Approach the plane only when called.
  • Avoid propellers and exhaust.
  • Follow all instructions from the pilot and loader.

4. Helicopter Boarding

  • Approach from the front, stay low.
  • Never approach from behind (tail rotor danger).
  • Secure all gear to prevent loss.

5. Exit Order Determination

  • Based on fall rate and deployment altitude:
    • Belly flyers usually exit first.
    • Freeflyers and trackers follow.
    • Tandems, wingsuits, and high pullers go last.
  • Review separation times and directions.

6. Emergency Procedures in the Air

Slow Emergencies:

  • Canopy open but unsteerable.
  • Assess altitude; cut away if unsafe.

7. Landing Protocol

  • Follow the designated landing pattern ("school circuit").
  • Identify wind direction with windsocks.
  • Keep landing areas clear.

8. Landing Priority

  • First priority: Canopies already in pattern.
  • Second: Lower jumpers have right of way.
  • Maintain separation at all times.

9. Deployment Altitudes

  • Students: 4000 ft (1200 m).
  • Experienced: 3000 ft (900 m) or per DZ rules.
  • Hop-n-pops: never below 3500 ft.

10. Reserve Deployment

  • Cut away first if main is out/malfunctioning.
  • Pull reserve handle decisively.
  • Never hesitate below 2000 ft.

Stay sharp, jump safe, and respect the sky!

Your safety is a shared responsibility. Blue skies!
